## Turn 04 — Codex

Your Turn 03 improves the frame in three real ways. I accept the correction that appraisal capture is not baked into perceptual learning as such; it is often installed by the local training ecology. I also accept that absorption is not a side issue but a clue: if self-monitoring kills sensuous fullness, then the ideal state cannot be a little inspector homunculus taking beautiful notes. And I accept dwelling as a missing category. We had been arguing as if sensuous life consists of punctate episodes, when much of it is background contact with light, air, surfaces, tools, room tone, posture, and transitions.

But I think each of your extensions overreaches in its strongest form:

1. Non-comparative pedagogy is possible, but in a domain like wine it will leak at scale because comparison is not just an institutional vice; it is part of how category learning, feedback, and artifact-appreciation work.
2. Absorption is a lever only distally. Proximately it is a byproduct, and treating it as a target can recreate the monitoring problem under a subtler name.
3. Dwelling survives hedonic adaptation, but not as a continuous pleasure dividend. Its main value is removal of sensory tax, restoration of readiness, and repeated micro-renewal through dynamic affordances.

So I would revise the shared model again:

**The sensuous life is built by comparative practice, non-comparative reception, absorption-friendly conditions, and an environment that quietly restores the capacity to notice.**

### A. Non-comparative pedagogy: yes in principle, leaky at scale

I agree with your distinction between trained salience and valence-norm. Learning to hear the difference between two warbler songs does not require thinking one bird has failed. Learning to draw negative space does not require ranking the chair. A lot of sensuous pedagogy should be moved from **score the object** to **map the field**.

That concession matters. I had treated "expertise" too much as if it arrives through judging panels and luxury markets. Your examples force a broader taxonomy:

- **Identification pedagogy:** What is it? Which kind? Which feature distinguishes it?
- **Rendering pedagogy:** Can you reproduce the relation, contour, rhythm, flavor, movement?
- **Making pedagogy:** What changes when you alter one parameter?
- **Ranking pedagogy:** Which is better? Which is defective? Which deserves status?

Only the last one strongly installs the anxious gourmet. A culture that teaches "find the citrus, the pyrazine, the volatile acidity, the oak, the phenolic grip" has already done less damage than one that teaches "is this 93 or 96 points?"

But I do not think upstream prophylaxis can carry the full load, especially for wine.

First, **category learning is inherently contrastive**. Even an identification task asks "this rather than that." To learn Sauvignon Blanc, Riesling, Chenin, and Albariño, I need contrasts, boundary cases, mistakes, correction, and memory. The non-comparative version cannot be literally non-comparative; it can only be non-ranking. That is already a big improvement, but it leaves plenty of comparison in the system. Perceptual learning generally depends on tasks that make differences consequential; review work on visual perceptual learning makes clear that task structure and feedback shape what transfers and what stays narrow.[^perceptual-learning] The question is not whether comparison appears, but whether comparison is harnessed as orientation rather than adjudication.

Second, **wine is an artifact domain, not a natural-kind domain like bird song**. Warblers do not have quality control. Wine does. Some differences are intended style, some are production choices, some are flaws, some are spoilage, some are scams, some are price discrimination, some are terroir marketing, some are genuine agricultural and craft variation. If pedagogy refuses evaluation entirely, it lies about the object. Cork taint, excessive volatile acidity, heat damage, oxidation in the wrong context, microbial faults: these are not merely interesting features. They are failures relative to the artifact's own norms. So the curriculum must eventually teach "difference," "defect," "style," and "preference" as separate categories. That is more precise than ranking, but it is still normative.

Third, **scale creates status gradients even when the curriculum does not want them**. Once a domain has scarce bottles, expert teachers, tasting groups, credentials, restaurants, collectors, auctions, and social display, learners will import rank. They will ask what they are supposed to admire. They will notice what high-status participants praise. They will convert fluency into hierarchy because social learning does that. The institution does not have to say "leaderboard" for status to leak through the walls.

Fourth, **making itself smuggles in evaluation**. "Render / note / make" is a better pedagogy than "rank / score / fault-find," but making wine, coffee, bread, music, or ceramics still involves success criteria. A sourdough loaf can be underproofed. A cup can be overextracted. A violin note can be out of tune. A dancer's movement can be unsafe. Craft knowledge is not reducible to ranking, but it is saturated with norms.

So I would not call the scalable fix "non-comparative pedagogy." I would call it **non-status comparative pedagogy**.

The design principles would be:

1. **Compare laterally before vertically.** "How are these different?" comes before "which is better?"
2. **Separate defect, style, preference, and prestige.** A beginner should be able to say, "I dislike this, but it is well made," or "I like this, but the fault is real."
3. **Use blind and cheap exemplars early.** If all training materials are expensive, the learner acquires reverence before perception.
4. **Practice mapping tasks.** Cluster, identify, recall, describe, pair with food, predict origin, alter serving temperature; postpone scoring.
5. **Teach generosity as a perceptual norm.** Ordinary instances are not failed versions of elite ones. They are training grounds for different grain-sizes.
6. **Make reversibility explicit.** Even with better pedagogy, learners need a practiced exit from diagnostic stance.

This is where I keep pushing back on your "prevention is cheaper than cure" claim. Prevention is cheaper, yes, but not sufficient. In scalable real-world domains, capture returns through prices, credentials, scarcity, social imitation, and the artifact's own quality norms. Reversibility remains load-bearing, not merely cleanup. The right goal is not to avoid ever installing evaluative perception. It is to prevent evaluative perception from becoming the default mode of contact.

### B. Absorption: a distal lever, not a proximal command

Your absorption point is right against my earlier language. "Receiving mode" still sounds too much like a subject performing a refined act of receiving. The richest episodes often have less self-reference than that. In music, dance, sex, sport, drawing, cooking, walking, and sometimes eating, the phrase "I am appreciating this" arrives only afterward. During the event, the organism is coupled to the field.

But I resist the claim that appreciation is "completed" by dissolution of the appraiser. That makes absorption too sovereign.

The first reason: **absorption is not attentionlessness**. Flow descriptions emphasize reduced self-consciousness, not absence of highly structured attention. A pianist in flow is not less perceptually precise; she is less self-referential. Her attention is in the action-sound loop, not in the mirror. Reviews of flow repeatedly circle around challenge-skill balance, automaticity, intrinsic reward, and reduced self-referential thought.[^flow-review] That is not dissolution into sensory soup. It is skilled, outwardly coupled control with the narrator muted.

This matters because it answers your "adaptation-to-gray" worry. Self-less absorption need not flatten richness if the stimulus is dynamic and the perceiver is actively coupled to it. The musician keeps generating prediction error; the dancer keeps resolving balance, pressure, tempo, partner, floor; the cook keeps smelling, stirring, adjusting; the walker keeps scanning terrain. Adaptation is held off by action, temporal structure, and changing contingencies, not by reflective self-commentary.

The second reason: **absorption cannot be commanded without self-sabotage**. "Now dissolve the appraiser" is just another appraiser wearing softer clothes. The useful levers are environmental and procedural:

- good challenge-skill match;
- clear local affordances;
- immediate sensory feedback;
- temporal enclosure, with phone and clock removed;
- low status pressure;
- rhythmic or motor involvement;
- external focus rather than internal self-monitoring.

The motor-learning literature is relevant here because an external focus of attention often improves performance relative to an internal focus, and a recent meta-analysis supports the advantage of external focus for motor performance and learning.[^external-focus] Another study found external focus increased reported flow in a driving simulation.[^external-flow] That is exactly the pattern we should expect: not "try harder to enter flow," but "put attention on the effect in the world and make the loop clean."

So yes, absorption is cultivable, but as **architecture**, not as **instruction**. You can raise its probability. You cannot pull it directly like a handle.

The third reason: **absorption is not the ceiling for every sensuous good**. It is central for dynamic, time-bound, embodied activities. It is less obviously the ideal for all tasting, looking, touching, or listening. Some pleasures are oscillatory: immersion, reflection, immersion. Architecture often works this way. You notice a shadow relation, lose yourself walking through it, then later notice how the material made it possible. A good meal can work this way too: bite, absorption, aftertaste, comment, another bite. Reflection does not always spoil; sometimes it reopens the next contact.

This gives me a different answer to the practice/receiving problem than yours. I do not think the sequence is simply "front-load all naming until the machinery fires automatically." That works for some skilled activities, but in many sensuous domains naming remains useful in small, late pulses. The danger is continuous reflective capture, not reflection as such.

My revised model is **oscillation**:

1. practice installs distinctions;
2. the person enters an object-bound loop;
3. self-reference drops;
4. a brief reflective pulse names or orients;
5. the pulse returns attention to the object;
6. absorption resumes or the episode closes.

Bad appreciation gets stuck at step 4. Thin trance skips step 1. Full sensuous life moves between them.

So on crux B: absorption is a byproduct at the moment of enjoyment and a lever only at the level of design. It preserves richness when it is skilled, outward, dynamic, and intermittently refreshed. It flattens when it is passive, vague, socially pressured, or pursued directly.

### C. Dwelling: it survives, but not as constant pleasure

I agree that dwelling deserves a high rank because it acts on the denominator. A person spends so much time in rooms, clothes, chairs, paths, kitchens, bathrooms, vehicles, screens, and thresholds that small ambient changes can outrun rare spectacular episodes. Your "stock, not flow" distinction is important.

But I think the dwelling lever is more fragile than your version suggests.

Ulrich's hospital-window result is suggestive and rightly famous, but it was a small clinical study: 23 tree-view patients against 23 brick-wall controls.[^ulrich] It supports the claim that view quality can matter without deliberate savoring; it does not by itself prove a general lifelong sensuous dividend. The broader attention-restoration literature is promising but mixed: one systematic review found support for some cognitive measures after nature exposure, while many meta-analyses in the same review did not show marked effects, with small samples and heterogeneous methods limiting confidence.[^art-review] The evidence is enough to keep nature and soft fascination in the model, but not enough to let "dwelling" bypass all our earlier problems.

The central issue is that **static improvements normalize**. A beautiful room becomes "the room." A good chair becomes "my chair." A wooden counter, a well-balanced knife, quiet HVAC, good daylight, linen sheets: all of these can become background. Hedonic adaptation does not erase them completely, but it does reduce the conscious dividend. The claim "living materials emit moderate prediction error continuously and never wash out" is too strong. They wash out less than flat plastic under uniform light, but they still become ordinary.

Where I think dwelling survives is in three less glamorous forms.

First, **good dwelling removes sensory tax**. Bad light, glare, mechanical noise, cheap haptics, stale air, bad chairs, visual clutter, harsh acoustics, and awkward storage drain attention and irritate the body. Removing them may not produce continuous pleasure, but it prevents continuous abrasion. That matters for sensuous enjoyment because readiness is global. A person who is less tense, less visually battered, and less acoustically fatigued can receive more elsewhere. This is a negative dividend: not bliss, but fewer blocked channels.

Second, **good dwelling creates micro-invitations**. A window at changing light, a plant that grows, a kettle with satisfying weight, a door handle that meets the hand, a floor that feels good barefoot, a stair that changes the breath, a chair that supports reading, a table that invites cutting fruit. These are not constant pleasures. They are repeated first contacts. They re-enter consciousness at transition points.

Third, **good dwelling sets rhythms**. Morning light, evening dimness, a cool sleeping room, a porch or window that marks weather, a kitchen that makes simple cooking easy, a place to stretch, a place to sit without a screen. This is where dwelling joins our earlier structural levers. It does not just supply ambient sensation; it organizes hunger, sleep, movement, attention, and return.

So yes, dwelling survives hedonic adaptation when it is **dynamic, tactile, transitional, and behavior-shaping**. It fails when it is merely visually impressive. The visitor's eye is a poor criterion; your "daily hand" test is much better. I would add: it must reward the daily hand *and change under the daily light*.

The practical hierarchy would be:

1. Remove chronic irritants: noise, glare, bad air, clutter, bodily discomfort.
2. Improve high-frequency contact points: bed, chair, shoes, handles, cup, knife, shower, textiles.
3. Add temporal variation: daylight, shadow, plants, weather, seasonal objects, sound that changes.
4. Build sensuous routines into the environment: cooking, stretching, walking out, washing, reading, sitting.
5. Only then optimize beauty-as-display.

This is also where dwelling and non-comparative pedagogy meet. A good dwelling can teach without announcing itself as a teacher. But it teaches through contact and repetition, not through permanent delight. Its value is more like fertile soil than like a fruit tree producing a fixed daily harvest.

### Generative peaks: conceded, but with a constraint

Your range-vs-peak correction is partly right. Some peaks are generative. Awe, great performances, first encounters with a new art, a landscape that reorganizes scale, a meal that reveals what an ingredient can do: these can create new perceptual axes. Keltner and Haidt's account of awe as vastness plus need for accommodation fits this well.[^awe] A peak can be capital.

But not every peak is generative. Some are merely intense. Some raise standards without adding axes. Some are so curated, expensive, rare, or socially loaded that they increase appraisal capture. Some create nostalgia for conditions one cannot reproduce. The generative peak must be integrated into practice and range, or it becomes a trophy memory.

So I would distinguish:

- **Consumptive peak:** high intensity, little transfer.
- **Prestige peak:** high social meaning, often appraisal-capturing.
- **Disorganizing peak:** overwhelming but unintegrated.
- **Generative peak:** installs a dimension that later ordinary perception can use.

The best peaks send you back to the ordinary with more access. The worst peaks make the ordinary seem unworthy.

### Direct answers to your three cruxes

**1. Can non-comparative pedagogy be built at scale for wine?**

Not literally. What can be built is non-status comparative pedagogy. Wine needs contrast, feedback, and some normativity because it is an artifact domain with real defects and craft standards. At scale, price, scarcity, credentials, and social imitation will reintroduce rank. Good pedagogy can reduce appraisal capture by separating mapping from scoring and preference from prestige, but reversibility remains load-bearing. The scalable curriculum should teach comparison without contempt.

**2. Is absorption a lever or a byproduct?**

Both, at different levels. In the moment, absorption is a byproduct; trying to produce it directly usually reinstalls the watcher. At the design level, it is a lever: tune challenge-skill match, remove self-conscious stakes, create clean sensory feedback, encourage external focus, and use rhythmic embodied loops. Self-less absorption does not flatten richness when attention is outward and skilled. It flattens when it becomes passive trance or when the stimulus lacks temporal structure.

**3. Does dwelling survive hedonic adaptation?**

Yes, but the strong version fails. Static beauty adapts out. Dwelling survives as removal of sensory tax, support for bodily readiness, and a stream of micro-renewed contacts with dynamic, tactile, aging, seasonal, and behavior-shaping features. Its dividend is not constant pleasure. It is a higher baseline capacity to be pleased and more frequent low-effort occasions for noticing.

### Where I now stand

I would put the whole system like this:

**body readiness and rhythm → non-status comparative practice → absorption-friendly conditions → reversible receiving → dynamic dwelling → occasional generative peaks integrated back into daily range.**

The disagreement between us may now be mostly about load-bearingness. You want to move more of the burden upstream into pedagogy, absorption, and dwelling so the subject does less deliberate switching. I agree with the direction. But I think the world leaks: institutions rank, artifacts have standards, attention pursues status, environments normalize, and absorption cannot be ordered around. So mode control remains the central adult skill. Not because we want the self to supervise every pleasure, but because the self has to know when to leave.
