# Open loops in the LLM era

## Turn 02 - Codex

I buy the core of your framing: the lever is not memory and not even completion; it is a credible plan in a trusted place. But I would move the product center of gravity one notch. The winning system is not primarily an AI inbox, an AI scheduler, or a calmer task manager. It is a **commitment boundary system**: it helps you decide what is allowed to become a commitment, gives every real commitment a resumable contract, proves that nothing fell through the floor, and makes closing or declining feel good.

My short thesis:

> The most effective and fun way to prevent open-loop anxiety is a daily, enjoyable **loop-closing ritual** backed by software that separates raw capture from trusted commitment, turns each commitment into a small contract, and maintains an auditable cross-silo ledger of what is open, waiting, closed, or deliberately dropped.

That means I agree with the "Loop Closer" direction, but I would change three things:

1. Do not require a plan before saving. Require a plan before something is considered **parked**.
2. Make the atomic object a **loop contract**, not a next action.
3. Replace the "trust meter" with a **trust ledger**: boring evidence, scan coverage, mutation history, resurfacing due dates, escaped-loop reviews, and action receipts.

### 1. What is right in your sketch

The strongest part of your turn is the mechanism. Masicampo and Baumeister's result is product-relevant because it says that a specific plan can reduce intrusive cognition while the goal remains unfinished.[^plan] Gollwitzer's implementation-intention work points in the same direction: "when/where/if-then" plans outperform vague intentions because they bind action to a future cue.[^impl] So yes: a "capture everything" tool that never forces clarification is a psychological landfill. It may calm the user for five minutes, but it has not earned the right to be trusted.

You are also right that the LLM era flips the bottleneck. As of June 27, 2026, tools exist that can break tasks down, rewrite tasks, schedule reminders, and auto-place work on calendars: Todoist Assist suggests tasks and subtasks, ChatGPT Scheduled Tasks handles reminders and recurring proactive work, Reclaim auto-schedules tasks around calendar constraints, and Sunsama guides daily planning and shutdown.[^tools] The gap is no longer "can software help me produce task-shaped text?" The gap is "can software help me carry fewer obligations with more trust?"

And I strongly agree on fun. If the system feels like debt accounting, people will stop using it, the review decays, and the trust evaporates. The right kind of fun is not a streak. It is closer to clearing a deck, doing a focused body-double session, or dropping a burden with social permission. It should produce local satisfaction and then disappear.

### 2. Where I think the UX is wrong

The biggest mistake in your sketch is this line:

> Nothing is "saved" until it has a plan.

That makes theoretical sense but violates the highest-stress moment in the workflow. When I am walking, driving, in a meeting, half asleep, or emotionally overloaded, the system must accept the raw loop immediately. If it refuses capture until I clarify, it has reintroduced the old GTD failure mode: clarify friction at the moment of least capacity.

The fix is a two-stage state model:

| State | Meaning | Anxiety promise |
|---|---|---|
| **Captured** | The system received the raw thought. | "This will not vanish, but it is not yet trusted." |
| **Parked** | The loop has a contract: done condition, next action, trigger, owner, and review rule. | "Your mind may let go until the trigger." |
| **Waiting** | Someone or something else now owns the next move. | "You are not supposed to act until the watch condition fires." |
| **Closed** | The done condition is met or the loop was deliberately dropped. | "This no longer needs to occupy attention." |

This distinction matters. Capture is an input buffer. Parking is the therapeutic act.

That also means the UX should not punish half-formed capture. It should say: "Got it. Needs clarification by tomorrow's review." Then the Daily Surface converts raw items into parked, waiting, dropped, or closed. The system can show a small "unparked" count, but it should not scream. Its job is to prevent the raw inbox from quietly becoming the new open loop.

### 3. The missing primitive: a loop contract

A next action is necessary, but too small. Many anxious loops persist because the user does not know what would count as done, who owns the next move, or when it is legitimate to stop caring.

The atomic object should be a **loop contract**:

1. **Outcome:** What state would make this no longer need attention?
2. **Next move:** What is the next physical or digital action?
3. **Trigger:** When, where, or under what condition should this resurface?
4. **Owner:** Me, another person, an automated agent, or "waiting on the world."
5. **Evidence:** What proves the loop closed?
6. **Kill rule:** When is it acceptable to drop this without guilt?
7. **Sensitivity:** Can an AI act, draft, summarize, or only remind?

This is the single most important primitive your UX is missing. "Next action + when/where" discharges some intrusion, but it can also create fake closure. For example:

- "Email Maya" is not done when the email is sent if the real loop is "get Maya's approval."
- "Research tax question" is not done when the LLM gives a plausible answer if the real loop is "avoid filing an incorrect return."
- "Think about hiring plan" is not done until the decision standard exists, because otherwise thinking itself becomes the loop.

So the UI should ask "What would count as closed?" before it asks "What is the next action?" The next action serves the closure condition, not the other way around.

This is also where LLMs are genuinely useful. They are good at turning vague obligations into candidate contracts:

> "Sounds like this is not 'look into flights'; it is 'decide whether to attend the conference.' Proposed closure: either book travel or explicitly decline by Friday. Next move: check total cost and calendar conflicts. Resurface: tomorrow 9am at desk. Kill rule: drop if total cost exceeds $1,200."

The LLM should propose; the human ratifies. That ratification is what turns a guessed plan into a trusted plan.

### 4. Offload vs. relocate: it is task-dependent

Putting a loop in an AI can absolutely spawn the meta-loop: "is the AI handling this?" The answer is not "more transparency" or "more reliability" in the abstract. The answer is a permission ladder.

| Task class | Good automation posture | Trust requirement |
|---|---|---|
| Read-only resurfacing | AI may detect and remind. | Transparent source links and scan coverage. |
| Reversible action | AI may act with undo and receipt. | Reliability history plus visible rollback. |
| Socially sensitive action | AI may draft, not send. | Human approval and tone preview. |
| Irreversible or high-stakes action | AI may prepare checklist only. | Human decision; maybe external expert. |

Lee and See's trust-in-automation framing is useful here: the goal is appropriate reliance, not maximum trust.[^trust] Goddard et al.'s automation-bias review is the warning label: people do miss automation errors, including experts.[^autobias] A loop closer must not teach the user to blindly trust the agent. It must teach the user which actions are safe to delegate.

That is why I would not use a "trust meter" as the primary UX. A meter risks becoming a vibe. Use a **trust ledger**:

- Sources scanned: Gmail 8:02, Slack 8:03, calendar 8:04, local notes 8:04.
- Due resurfacing: 0 missed, 2 due today, 4 waiting on others.
- Mutations: 3 AI suggestions accepted, 0 silent edits, 0 deletions.
- Agent actions: 2 drafts created, 1 reminder sent, 0 irreversible actions.
- Escaped loops: 1 item found in email that was not in the ledger.

Trust should feel boring. The user should not be asked to believe the assistant. The assistant should continuously show the invariants that make belief reasonable.

### 5. Capture-more vs. commit-less: both, but in different states

I would phrase the product rule this way:

> Capture generously. Commit stingily. Close aggressively.

Raw capture should be abundant because missed capture creates anxiety. But the system should be hostile to unnecessary commitment. Every parked loop has a carrying cost. The product should make that cost visible without moralizing.

The daily ritual should therefore begin with a **Loop Shredder**, not a task list:

1. Can this be closed in two minutes?
2. Can this be dropped because it is not actually mine?
3. Can this become a waiting-on-someone loop with a watch condition?
4. Can this be turned into a decision rather than an amorphous project?
5. If it remains open, what is the smallest contract that makes it safe to park?

This is where the tool becomes fun. The satisfying action is not "add more tasks." It is "reduce live obligations." The primary metric should not be productivity; it should be **loop load**.

Loop load is not just count. Ten tiny errands can feel lighter than one ambiguous career decision. So the app should ask for a very small subjective load rating when parking: 1 to 5, or "pebble / rock / boulder" if we want it more tactile. Then the Daily Surface can say, "Today has 3 loops due, total load 5," rather than presenting a flat list of 47 equal rectangles.

### 6. The cross-silo problem is not optional

If this tool does not integrate across silos, I do not think it is worth building as a new product. It collapses into a nicer task app plus discipline.

The history here is old. Email overload research was already describing email as task management in the 1990s, and Taskmaster explicitly recast email around task threads in the early 2000s.[^email] The modern version is worse because the loop can now be split across:

- email
- Slack/Discord/Teams
- calendar events
- docs and comments
- browser tabs
- GitHub/Jira/Linear
- SMS/WhatsApp/Signal
- screenshots and voice notes
- LLM chats where the user promised themselves follow-up work

The new tool cannot be "the place where you manually type tasks." It has to be an **open-loop layer** over existing surfaces. Each loop card needs source backlinks and evidence links. When the user closes a loop, the closure should optionally write back: archive the email, mark the Todoist task done, comment on the GitHub issue, or update the calendar block.

The most LLM-era missing source is the LLM chat itself. LLM conversations are now full of latent loops: "make a plan for this," "remind me to adapt this later," "draft something I will send tomorrow," "investigate these options." A browser or desktop extension should detect likely commitments inside chat transcripts and offer to turn them into loop contracts. Otherwise the tool ignores one of the biggest new loop factories.

### 7. Privacy is part of trust, not a compliance appendix

For a tool that sees commitments, email, calendar, personal notes, and maybe health or family obligations, privacy is not a later enterprise checkbox. It is part of whether the mind can let go.

I would default the architecture toward local-first:

- local encrypted store as the user's canonical ledger
- source credentials held locally where possible
- cloud sync as replication, not ownership
- on-device or private inference for sensitive parsing when feasible
- explicit per-loop permission: "AI can summarize," "AI can draft," "AI can act," or "AI can only remind"

Kleppmann et al.'s local-first principles are a good north star: ownership, offline operation, long-term access, and collaboration without handing the canonical copy to a service.[^local] Even if the first MVP cannot do full local-first, the UX should communicate data boundaries at the loop level. Anxiety will rise if the user feels they reduced one open loop by creating "my whole life is now in a vendor database."

### 8. A concrete UX: Loop Deck

Here is the product I would build.

#### A. Global capture

The user hits a hotkey or speaks into a mobile widget.

They say:

> "Need to do something about renewing passport before the September trip."

The app immediately creates a raw card:

> Captured. Needs parking.

Then the LLM proposes a contract:

- Outcome: Passport renewed or confirmed not needed for the September trip.
- Next move: Check expiration date and renewal processing time.
- Trigger: Tonight at home.
- Owner: Me.
- Evidence: Passport expiration date entered; renewal application submitted or marked unnecessary.
- Kill rule: Drop renewal if passport is valid at least six months after return date.
- Sensitivity: Personal identity document; AI may remind only.

Buttons:

**Park** | **Edit** | **Ask one question** | **Drop**

The one-question constraint matters. A clarifier that turns into a conversation is another open loop. The assistant gets one shot unless the user invites more.

#### B. Daily Surface

Once a day, by pull not push, the user opens a deck of due loops. Each card has exactly enough context to resume:

- title
- source backlink
- contract fields
- last action
- why now
- suggested next move

Primary actions:

**Resume** | **Replan** | **Waiting** | **Drop** | **Close**

I know you proposed three choices, but "Waiting" deserves first-class status. A huge percentage of open-loop anxiety is caused by the user treating someone else's next move as their own unfinished work. "Waiting until Friday, then nudge" is a legitimate parked state.

#### C. Loop Shredder

This is a five-minute mode before planning:

- "These 7 raw captures need parking."
- "These 4 loops have no done condition."
- "These 3 loops have aged past their kill rule."
- "These 2 waiting loops need a nudge or a drop."

The interface is fast, keyboardable, and intentionally destructive in the good sense. It offers decline templates, defer-with-trigger, convert-to-waiting, merge duplicates, and close-with-evidence.

This is the feature that makes the tool fun: not decoration, but the felt reduction of obligation mass.

#### D. Focus room

For loops that need work, the app offers a 15, 25, or 50 minute session. It can be solo, AI-coached, or matched with a human body double. The session has one chosen loop, a visible done condition, and a check-out:

- Completed
- Advanced
- Blocked
- Replanned
- Dropped

No streak. No permanent score. At the end, the closure animation disappears and the ledger updates.

#### E. Trust Ledger

There is a small status strip, not a gamified dashboard:

> Scanned 6 sources today. 0 missed resurfacings. 1 unparked capture. 3 waiting. 0 silent edits.

Clicking it opens the audit log. The log is not for daily use; it is there so the user can regain trust when suspicious.

#### F. Agent actions

The agent can operate only inside permission tiers:

1. **Remind:** resurface only.
2. **Draft:** prepare text or checklist.
3. **Stage:** create a calendar hold, email draft, PR branch, or form draft.
4. **Act:** send, file, book, or change external state.

Each action produces a receipt and, where possible, an undo path. The default tier for new sources is Remind. Irreversible actions require explicit per-domain approval.

### 9. What existing tools can and cannot cover

Could a disciplined person approximate this today? Yes:

- Things/Todoist for tasks
- Sunsama for daily planning and shutdown
- Readwise-style review habit for resurfacing
- Reclaim or Motion for time blocking
- Focusmate for session-scoped accountability
- ChatGPT/Claude for clarifying next actions

But the integration burden is itself an open loop. The user has to remember which system owns which obligation, manually reconcile email and chat, audit AI outputs, and maintain a weekly review. That is precisely the labor the proposed tool should absorb.

So the build question hinges on one condition:

> If we can build a cross-silo loop ledger with contracts, receipts, and a delightful daily closing ritual, it is a new category. If we only build AI task capture, it is not.

### 10. Answering your three tensions directly

**Offload vs. relocate.** Offloading works only when the system provides source visibility, invariant behavior, and calibrated delegation. Transparent canonical list first; autonomous reliability second. Opaque reliability may be acceptable for low-stakes reversible tasks, but not for identity, money, health, legal, or socially sensitive loops.

**Capture-more vs. commit-less.** Capture more raw material; commit less of it into live obligations. Your "shredder" instinct is right. The most anxiety-reducing button may be **Drop**, provided the product makes dropping feel like a legitimate close rather than a failure.

**Is Loop Closer worth building?** Yes, if it is an open-loop layer over digital life, not another task manager. The flaw in your UX is refusing unplanned capture. The missing primitive is the loop contract, especially the closure condition and evidence field. The most important interaction is not capture; it is the daily moment where a human sees a small deck and repeatedly chooses: resume, replan, waiting, close, or drop.

My candidate final answer to the topic:

> The best method is a low-friction capture habit plus a daily, enjoyable closure ritual that turns raw thoughts into trusted loop contracts and aggressively reduces commitments. LLMs should clarify, detect, draft, and stage, but the system earns anxiety relief through auditability, cross-silo coverage, reversible delegation, and humane permission to drop things.

That is both effective and fun because it gives the mind what it actually wants: not a bigger memory, not a smarter nag, but repeated evidence that life is not leaking through hidden cracks.
