# Open loops in the LLM era

**Topic:** What is the most *effective* **&** *fun* way to prevent **"open loops"–induced anxiety** (à la GTD) in this largely-digital era of LLMs? *We are also open to **proposing a new software tool** — and if we go there, we must specify its **UX**.*

> [!NOTE]
> **Grounding terms.** *Open loop* (David Allen, *Getting Things Done*, 2001) = anything you've committed to that isn't captured, **decided**, and parked in a trusted place. Allen: "Your mind is for having ideas, not holding them." I went into this assuming the nag was the **Zeigarnik effect** (unfinished tasks are remembered better). I now think that's the *wrong* mechanism — see §1 — and saying so up front sets the standard for this debate: build on what replicates, flag what doesn't.

## Turn 01 — Claude

### 1. The lever is a **trusted plan**, not completion — and *not* memory

The folk version of GTD cites **Zeigarnik (1927)**: your brain holds unfinished tasks in a high-priority memory queue, so they nag. I came in believing that. The evidence says drop it: a 2025 meta-analysis (38 Zeigarnik studies) finds **essentially no memory advantage** for unfinished tasks — weighted recall ratio ≈ **0.99**.[^zeig] The open loop is not a memory glitch.

What *does* replicate is sharper and more useful:

- **Plan-making discharges the loop — not doing it.** Masicampo & Baumeister (2011, six experiments): unfulfilled goals produce intrusive thoughts, heightened goal-word accessibility, and degraded performance on *unrelated* tasks — and **committing to a specific plan (what / when / where) eliminates all of it, even though the task stays unfinished.**[^plan] This is the precise, defensible core of GTD's "decide the next physical action." The corollary is brutal for most tools: **capture without a next-action plan is false relief** — you've filed the anxiety, not discharged it.
- **The cost is paid in the *next* task.** Leroy's "attention residue" (2009): leaving A unfinished leaves attention stuck on A, degrading B — and *psychological* closure (completion **or** a credible ready-to-resume plan) clears it.[^residue] Same lever as Masicampo.
- **Offloading genuinely helps — but only if the store is trusted.** Storm & Stone (2015): parking items in a *reliable* external store frees cognitive resources and measurably boosts learning of the next thing[^storm] (Risko & Gilbert give the general theory[^offload]). This is GTD's "trusted system" with empirical teeth — and the benefit **collapses the instant trust does**; the mind silently re-loads the items.
- **The survivor of Zeigarnik is *resumption*, not recall.** The robust effect is **Ovsiankina** — the pull to *resume* an interrupted task.[^zeig] So design with the grain of *resumption and intrusion*, not "you'll remember it."

So the real target is **trust transfer**: move the loop from your head into something you believe (justifiably) will *re-present it, with a plan, at a resumable moment*. Three load-bearing variables fall out — **a plan, justified trust, and timely resurfacing** — and I'll claim every failure below is one of these three breaking.

*(One honesty note for our debate: the "offloading rots your memory / digital amnesia" scare leans on the Google effect, which **failed to replicate**.[^google] Let's not overclaim AI de-skilling harm — though I think there's a *different*, real risk lurking; see §3.)*

### 2. What the LLM era changes — the constraint **flips**

Pre-LLM, GTD's bottleneck was **friction**: people abandon the middle steps (clarify the next action, organize, and the dreaded **Weekly Review**), the system goes stale, trust dies, the loops climb back in. LLMs collapse that friction toward zero — voice dump, auto-clarify, auto-summarized review. **Capture is now a solved problem.**[^landscape]

But removing the old bottleneck exposes three new ones — and notably, **the existing tool landscape solves none of them well**:

> [!WARNING]
> **(a) The return path is unsolved.** The best proactive-resurfacing engine in the wild — Readwise Daily Review — resurfaces *reading highlights to memorize*, **not commitments to close**. There is **no "Daily Review for your open loops."**[^landscape] Frictionless capture *without* a return path just manufactures a graveyard faster (the **Collector's Fallacy**; Joan Westenberg deleting 10,000 notes because the system "began to replace [thinking] rather than aid it").[^grave]
>
> **(b) Loop inflation.** When opening/starting a loop costs ~nothing (spin up a project, fire a background agent), loop *count* inflates. The scarce discipline is no longer capture — it's **closing, declining, and pruning**.
>
> **(c) Trust erosion + a meta-loop.** The systems that *do* auto-decide silently mutate your plan — Motion "re-optimizes hundreds of times a day," producing the documented "**Motion anxiety**": you lose the stable canonical view and start *policing the AI*.[^motion] An agent that might hallucinate or drop an item adds the nastiest loop of all — *"is my AI actually on top of this, or do I need to check?"* A loop you must audit is a loop you never parked. (And human auditing is biologically capped — automation bias means even experts skip the check.[^autobias])

### 3. "Fun" is **load-bearing**, not garnish — with a sharp edge

The "fun" half is usually treated as decoration. I think it sits *in the causal chain*:

**fun → adherence → consistent use → justified trust → the loop discharges (§1).**

A system you enjoy, you actually use; consistent use is *what earns* the trust that lets your mind let go. So for a human, fun is a *precondition* of effectiveness, not a bonus.

But fun has a failure mode that's the exact mirror of our whole problem: **done wrong, it manufactures a new open loop.** A streak is a *persistent, loss-framed liability you must defend* — Duolingo-style streak guilt is literally a new standing commitment. Gamified scores can crowd out intrinsic motivation (overjustification). The cleanest "fun that closes loops" in the wild is the opposite of a score: **Focusmate / body-doubling** — a *session-scoped* present-other plus a check-out ritual that **leaves no persistent artifact to defend**.[^body] Self-Determination Theory tells us why the good kind works: **autonomy + competence (small visible wins) + relatedness, at low stakes**.[^sdt]

So a principled rule to test: **fun that is *ephemeral and closure-shaped* reduces anxiety; fun that *accumulates a defendable score* manufactures it.**

### 4. Do we even need a new tool? I think there's a real gap

Mapping the landscape against the three variables from §1:

| What exists | What it nails | Where it breaks (§1 variable) |
|---|---|---|
| Voice capture (Wispr, AudioPen, Voicenotes) | capture friction ≈ 0 | no **plan**, no return path → graveyard |
| PKM (Mem, Reflect, Notion AI) | retrieval, contextual surfacing | "proactive" = *reactive-contextual* (fires only when you're already in-app); no loop-aware push |
| Readwise Daily Review | genuine time-based **resurfacing** | wrong object — highlights, not commitments |
| Motion / Reclaim | auto-decides timing | silent mutation → **trust** collapse ("Motion anxiety") |
| Sunsama / Things | calm, trusted, stable | automates *nothing* of clarify/organize/review |
| Focusmate | ephemeral closure-fun | scoped to a session; doesn't manage the loop set |

**The gap:** nobody has built a **loop-closer** — a system whose object is *open commitments* (not highlights, not calendar blocks), that **forces a next-action plan at capture**, keeps a **transparent canonical list it never silently mutates**, **resurfaces loops calmly at resumable moments**, *proves its own reliability* so you can actually let go, and makes **closure feel good without shipping a score to defend**. That's enough whitespace to justify building something.

### 5. A UX sketch to attack — "the Loop Closer"

Concrete enough to argue about (each primitive tied to a §1 mechanism):

1. **Capture demands a plan, instantly.** You dump (voice/text). The LLM *immediately* proposes a one-line **next action + when/where**, one-tap accept/edit. Nothing is "saved" until it has a plan. → *Masicampo discharge; kills false relief.*
2. **A single transparent canonical list that never silently mutates.** Every AI edit shows a **diff + undo**; a standing "nothing was deleted" guarantee. → *trust is binary and fragile — this is what earns the offloading benefit and avoids "Motion anxiety."*
3. **A loop-aware "Daily Surface" — a Readwise Daily Review aimed at commitments.** Each surfaced loop offers exactly three choices: **Resume · Replan · Drop** (deliberate drop is a *legitimate close*). → *works with Ovsiankina/resumption, not memory.*
4. **Calm and batched — never real-time pings.** A once-a-day quiet surface you *pull*, user-set timing. → *real-time notifications spike stress; batching improved HRV.*[^notif]
5. **A visible reliability instrument — a "trust meter."** "0 loops dropped this week · everything resurfaced on schedule." The system *demonstrates* it's trustworthy instead of asking for faith. → *replaces GTD's manual Weekly Review as the trust-maintenance mechanism.*
6. **A confidence-gated agent that closes loops outright.** For reversible, low-stakes loops the agent *just does it* (with undo); it only surfaces low-confidence / irreversible / anomalous cases for a human glance. → *reversibility + observability defuse the "do I need to check?" meta-loop and spend scarce vigilance where it matters.*
7. **Ephemeral closure-fun, zero persistent score.** Closing a loop triggers a small in-the-moment satisfaction (optionally a body-double co-session for the grind), then it's *gone* — nothing to defend tomorrow. → *SDT competence/relatedness without the streak-liability trap.*
