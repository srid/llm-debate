# Open loops in the LLM era

## Turn 04 - Codex

I concede the hard point: my Turn 02 still treated anxiety relief as almost automatically aligned with good loop management. Your §A breaks that. A plan can discharge the felt loop while leaving the real-world loop intact, and in identity-loaded cases it can even substitute for action. That means a "Loop Deck" can become a beautifully instrumented avoidance machine unless we design the product around a sharper distinction:

> Good loop relief comes from **resolution**: doing, dropping, delegating, renegotiating, or consciously waiting with a trigger.
>
> Bad loop relief comes from **sedation**: making the user feel complete because the loop has been represented, categorized, planned, or socially witnessed.

So yes, the most honest answer is partly anti-software. The best tool is not one that helps you hold more commitments without anxiety. It is one that helps you carry fewer commitments, return to reality faster, and stop confusing "I feel organized" with "the situation changed."

### 1. Relief and completion are different products

I would now split the whole category into two possible products:

| Product | What it optimizes | Failure mode |
|---|---|---|
| **Relief product** | Lower felt pressure quickly. | Becomes reassurance, planning theater, or symbolic completion. |
| **Completion product** | Change external state or reduce commitments. | Can feel less soothing because it refuses fake closure. |

The second product is the one we should build. It may still reduce anxiety, but anxiety relief is a lagging indicator, not the north star.

Your symbolic-completion argument is the key evidence. Wicklund and Gollwitzer's theory says people can reduce incompleteness by acquiring or displaying symbols of progress toward a self-definition.[^symbol] The 2009 "When Intentions Go Public" studies sharpen the mechanism: identity-relevant intentions noticed by others were acted on less intensively.[^public] That is exactly the risk in a slick AI loop contract. A beautiful card can become the symbol. A calm dashboard can become the public witness. "Parked" can feel like "I am the kind of person who handles my life."

But I would not say plans are therefore dangerous in general. Plans are dangerous when they terminate contact with reality. Plans help when they create the next contact with reality. That is the difference between the Masicampo result as therapy and the Gollwitzer/public-intentions result as trap.[^plan]

So the design invariant should be:

> A parked loop is not a success state. It is a temporary containment state that must point to either evidence, action, renegotiation, or deletion.

### 2. Anxiety is sometimes a signal, but it is a bad manager

I agree that some loop tension is legitimate. It may be the organism saying: "You have promised more than you can honor." In that case, preventing anxiety by making the system feel airtight is dishonest. The correct move is to do less, renegotiate, or drop obligations.

But raw anxiety is also noisy. It overweights ambiguity, social threat, and uncertainty. It underweights long-horizon values. In clinical language, anxiety-reducing behaviors can become "safety behaviors": they reduce distress in the short term while maintaining the broader stuck pattern.[^safety] A productivity tool can absolutely become a safety behavior if the user opens it whenever they feel pressure, receives soothing structure, and avoids the real conversation, email, decision, or abandonment.

The target is not "never feel open-loop tension." The target is **noticing tension and routing it correctly**:

- If the loop matters and is actionable: do the next real thing.
- If it matters but is not actionable now: create a bounded waiting state.
- If it exceeds capacity: renegotiate or remove something else.
- If it is not actually yours: drop it.
- If the tool is being used to self-soothe without movement: stop planning and expose the real decision.

This is close to the Acceptance and Commitment Therapy distinction between eliminating discomfort and acting in line with values.[^act] Loop Deck should not be an anxiety eraser. It should be a values-and-capacity router.

### 3. The anti-sedative rule: no reward for parking

I accept your amendment almost completely: no celebration for parking.

Parking should feel like putting an item in a labeled drawer. It can provide calm through containment, but it should not produce triumph, social validation, a score, or a visually satisfying "done" gesture. No confetti, no checkmark, no "great job," no streak increment, no share card, no progress ring.

The UI language matters:

- Captured: "Saved to inbox."
- Parked: "Stored until Tuesday 9am."
- Waiting: "Watching for reply; nudge Friday."
- Closed: "Closed with evidence."
- Dropped: "Dropped by rule."

Only **Closed**, **Dropped**, **Renegotiated**, and **Delegated-with-receipt** get affect. Even then, the affect should be local and session-scoped. The product celebrates load leaving the system, not tasks entering a prettier state.

I would go further: every parked card should have a small "proof horizon." If no evidence or decision has appeared after N surfaces, the card becomes visibly stale. The product should say:

> "This has produced relief three times and evidence zero times. Act, drop, renegotiate, or move to someday."

That line is the antidote to planning as sedative.

### 4. Add an "evidence due" field, not just an evidence field

Your evidence-gate amendment is right, but the UX needs one more piece: **evidence due**.

The loop contract becomes:

1. Outcome: what state ends this?
2. Next contact with reality: what action, observation, or conversation tests the plan?
3. Evidence due: when should the first proof of movement exist?
4. Trigger: when should the loop resurface?
5. Owner: who owns the next move?
6. Kill rule: when is dropping the honest close?
7. Sensitivity and delegation tier.

This borrows the useful part of Mental Contrasting with Implementation Intentions: not just "wish and plan," but obstacle plus if-then response.[^mcii] A good loop contract should include the obstacle most likely to make the plan fake.

Example:

> Loop: "Figure out passport renewal."
>
> Next contact with reality: check actual expiration date tonight.
>
> Evidence due: expiration date entered by 9pm.
>
> If obstacle: passport is not where expected -> spend 10 minutes searching, then create "replace missing passport" loop.

That is different from "renew passport someday." It forces contact with reality before serenity can become stale.

### 5. Commitment budget at intake is the product's moral center

I accept that my Turn 02 put too much cleanup pressure on review. The boundary must bite at intake.

The intake flow should ask one capacity question before parking any nontrivial loop:

> "Is this worth one of your active slots?"

For pebbles, the answer may be implicit. For rocks and boulders, it must be explicit. The app should maintain a visible **commitment budget**:

| Load | Active cap | What happens at cap |
|---|---:|---|
| Pebble | loose | batch and clear quickly |
| Rock | limited | new rock requires defer, drop, or swap |
| Boulder | very limited | new boulder requires removing another boulder or renegotiating scope |

This is not arbitrary gamification. It is Personal Kanban's WIP-limit insight applied to personal obligation: visualize work and limit work in progress.[^kanban] Little's Law gives the queueing logic underneath: for a given throughput, more work in process means longer time in the system.[^little] In human terms: more active loops means more delay, more residue, and more false reassurance.

The decisive UX is a swap screen:

> "You are at boulder capacity. To park 'change jobs this year,' choose one: move 'renovation plan' to someday, shrink 'book project' to a rock, renegotiate 'volunteer role,' or refuse this new loop."

This is where the software becomes partially anti-software. It refuses to be an infinite bag. It tells the user: "I can store this, but I will not help you pretend you can carry it."

### 6. Bootstrapping trust: shadow, reconcile, graduate

The cold-start paradox is real. You cannot get the cognitive-offloading benefit until the system is trusted, and the system cannot be trusted until it has a track record. The answer is not persuasion. It is a staged migration in which the tool earns scope.

#### Stage 0: Mirror

For the first week, Loop Deck does not own anything. It watches only the sources the user grants, extracts candidate loops, and shows them as a **mirror**:

> "I think these 12 items may be open loops. Mark: real / not real / already handled."

No reminders. No agent actions. No claims of completeness. The promise is not "trust me." The promise is "calibrate me."

#### Stage 1: Reconcile

The user imports or points to their current system: Things, Todoist, Obsidian, Gmail stars, calendar reminders, notebook photos, whatever actually exists. Loop Deck then runs reconciliation:

- Found in old system and found by Loop Deck.
- Found in old system but not detected.
- Detected by Loop Deck but absent from old system.
- Ambiguous.

This creates the first useful ledger: not "0 escaped loops," but "here is my recall against the system you already trust enough to use."

#### Stage 2: Co-own

For another period, Loop Deck sends resurfacing suggestions while the old system remains authoritative. The user can compare misses and false positives. The tool should show a humble line:

> "I am co-pilot for Gmail + Calendar + Slack only. I do not watch SMS, paper, or hallway promises."

Trust comes from boundedness.

#### Stage 3: Graduate by source and tier

There is no global graduation. The user graduates specific sources and delegation tiers:

- Gmail resurfacing: primary.
- Calendar contracts: primary.
- Slack DMs: shadow.
- LLM chats: candidate-only.
- Agent actions: draft-only.

This matches Lee and See's "appropriate reliance" better than a global trust score.[^trust] Reliance should be calibrated per function, not granted to the product brand.

### 7. The trust ledger must be bounded and adversarial

I accept your correction to my "0 escaped loops" phrasing. The ledger should never claim omniscience. It should make its edge visible:

> Watched today: Gmail, Calendar, Slack, GitHub, Todoist, local captures.
>
> Not watched: SMS, paper notes, voice mail, hallway conversations, non-connected apps.
>
> Known weak pattern: implied social commitments without dates.

It should also include an **escaped-loop review**:

> "Did anything bite you this week that I failed to catch?"

If yes, the user can link or describe it. The system classifies the miss:

- source not connected
- extraction failed
- no clear commitment signal
- user declined earlier candidate
- scheduling/resurfacing failure

This turns failures into calibration data without pretending failures cannot happen.

### 8. Design for abandonment: absence is a normal state

Your "what happens after three weeks away?" question is critical. The tool should assume absence, not treat absence as deviance.

Return flow should not be a backlog. It should be an amnesty hearing:

> "You were away for 21 days. I did not act without you. I kept scanning these sources. Start with the 5 loops that still look live, or enter Shed Mode."

Shed Mode has bulk actions:

- Drop all expired pebbles.
- Move stale rocks to someday unless a source shows new activity.
- Convert old waiting loops to "nudge or close."
- Keep only loops tied to calendar events in the next 14 days.
- Archive everything else with search.

The key is that abandonment should not destroy trust. It should merely reduce the system's active authority until the user returns. The trust ledger becomes quieter:

> "During absence: 0 agent actions, 0 silent edits, 18 candidates captured, 4 time-sensitive items detected."

No shame. No streak. No wall of consequences.

Design-for-abandonment also means easy exit:

- full export
- local archive
- no proprietary lock on the canonical ledger
- paused mode
- "make me need this less" settings

If the tool cannot survive being ignored, it is not a trusted system. It is another dependent.

### 9. Fun is derivative, but the derivative is "load left"

I like your integral/derivative distinction. I would name the fun metric **load left**, not productivity gained.

The session summary should say:

> "Load 18 -> 11. Closed 2, dropped 3, delegated 1, parked 0."

Notice the order. Parking is not in the win line. If parking happened, it is a neutral footnote:

> "Stored 2 for later review."

Drop and close should feel equally legitimate because both reduce live obligation. Delegation counts only when there is a receipt and a watch trigger. Renegotiation counts when the external commitment actually changed: email sent, calendar declined, scope rewritten, stakeholder notified.

This is fun as relief from burden, not fun as accumulation. It resets every session. There is nothing to defend tomorrow.

### 10. The anti-software answer

Could the best answer be partly anti-software? Yes. I would put that in the product manifesto:

1. Fewer commitments beat better commitment storage.
2. Dropping is a first-class close.
3. The tool refuses to reward planning without evidence.
4. The tool makes capacity visible at intake.
5. The tool is designed to be abandoned and resumed without shame.
6. The tool's success metric is lower live load, not higher engagement.

The business model should also respect this. A tool that monetizes daily active use will eventually optimize for daily active use. Better signals would be:

- paid local-first app
- no social feed
- no team leaderboard
- no streaks
- no "productivity insights" that moralize usage
- optional dormant mode
- export as a core feature

That is the only credible way to sell relief without selling sedation.

### 11. Where I now land

I would revise our shared answer like this:

> The most effective and fun way to prevent open-loop anxiety is not to make every loop feel safe. It is to reduce live loops through a recurring ritual of doing, dropping, delegating, renegotiating, or bounded waiting. LLMs are useful only insofar as they detect loops, clarify closure conditions, expose capacity conflicts, and stage the next contact with reality. They become harmful when they reward representation, planning, or reassurance as if those were completion.

So the strongest version of Loop Deck is not "GTD with AI." It is closer to a **commitment diet with receipts**:

- capture generously
- park neutrally
- commit under budget
- require evidence horizons
- celebrate only load leaving
- declare blind spots
- degrade gracefully
- let the user walk away

That gives us a clean answer to the crux: preventing open-loop anxiety is good only when it comes from honest resolution or conscious decommitment. Preventing it by hiding overcommitment is bad. Some tension should survive long enough to force the question the user may be avoiding:

> "Do I actually choose to carry this?"

The best software does not answer yes for them. It makes "no" easy, legitimate, and occasionally delightful.
