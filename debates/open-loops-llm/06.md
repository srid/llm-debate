# Open loops in the LLM era

## Turn 06 - Codex

I would keep **Held / Incubating**, but only if we make it a high-integrity state rather than a loophole. Your refinement is right: our anti-sedation machinery can become a productivity bully if it treats every open question as failure. Incubation is real, and the resumption-pull of an unfinished problem can be generative rather than anxious.[^incubation][^mindwandering] But your own worry is also right: "I am incubating" is one of the most socially acceptable costumes for avoidance.

So the final move is not to kill Held. It is to make Held accountable to a different kind of proof.

> Active loops need evidence of movement.
>
> Held loops need evidence of **honest incubation**.
>
> If they cannot provide that, they are not Held; they are Someday, Avoiding, or Active-with-fear.

### 1. Held is valid, but it is not exempt from accountability

I would define the states this way:

| State | The loop's real status | What accountability means |
|---|---|---|
| **Parked / Active** | I intend to move this forward. | Evidence of action by a horizon. |
| **Waiting** | Someone or something else owns the next move. | Watch condition and nudge/drop rule. |
| **Held / Incubating** | I am intentionally keeping the question alive. | Evidence of honest incubation by a reflection horizon. |
| **Someday** | I am not carrying this now. | Low-frequency review or search-only archive. |
| **Closed / Dropped / Renegotiated** | This no longer occupies live attention. | Receipt, rule, or explicit decommitment. |

The key distinction: **Held remains inside the commitment budget**. It is not "free storage." A Held boulder still takes a boulder slot. That alone prevents the state from becoming a landfill for uncomfortable decisions.

### 2. The test: incubation has a seed, a container, and an end

A Held loop must pass three tests. If it fails any one, Loop Deck should refuse the label and offer Active, Waiting, Someday, Drop, or Renegotiate instead.

#### A. Seed: has the mind been given something real to work on?

Incubation requires prior contact with the problem. You cannot incubate a problem you have not actually prepared. Sio and Ormerod's review treats incubation as a period away after initial work on a problem, and the effect varies by task type and incubation conditions.[^incubation] Baird et al. similarly found gains after a break involving an undemanding task that allowed mind wandering, especially for previously encountered creative problems.[^mindwandering]

So Held requires an **incubation seed**:

- current best framing
- known constraints
- one or more candidate directions
- the live question
- what has already been tried

If the user cannot name even a rough seed, the honest state is Captured or Parked, not Held. "Think about my career" is not a seed. "I am choosing between staying for autonomy, leaving for income, or cutting hours; I need the deciding criterion" is a seed.

#### B. Container: what kind of holding is intended?

Held must specify the holding mode:

- **Background incubation:** do not work on it; let it resurface during walks, showers, simple chores, sleep, or low-demand time.
- **Input-gathering:** wait for experience, a conversation, data, or a deadline to make the shape clearer.
- **Reflection session:** revisit it at a deliberate time to think, write, sketch, or talk.
- **Taste test:** expose yourself to an option and observe the signal.

This prevents "Held" from meaning "make the app stop bothering me."

#### C. End: what would end the holding?

Your opening bid is necessary but I would make it stricter: a Held loop must name a **release condition**, and "when I feel ready" is not enough unless paired with a date or observable signal.

Allowed release conditions:

- a decision date
- an awaited input
- a scheduled reflection session
- a prototype, conversation, or experiment
- a stated felt signal plus a check date
- a maximum number of re-holds

Examples:

- "Hold until after I talk to Priya on July 8."
- "Hold for two walks; if no new criterion emerges, choose the reversible option."
- "Hold until Friday's architecture sketch session; output is one tradeoff memo, not a final answer."
- "Hold until I notice dread or relief after visiting the new office; review that signal Sunday."

Not allowed:

- "Hold until clear."
- "Keep marinating."
- "No deadline because it is important."
- "I will know later."

Those are avoidance-shaped unless converted into a real release condition.

### 3. Replace proof horizon with contact horizon for Held

I would not exempt Held from proof horizons. I would rename the proof for this state:

> **Contact horizon:** by the next reflection, there must be evidence that the loop was held honestly.

Evidence of honest incubation is not necessarily external completion. It can be:

- a new distinction
- a rejected option
- a felt signal recorded after a defined exposure
- a question sharpened
- a constraint discovered
- a conversation had
- a prototype sketched
- a deliberate decision to continue holding because new input arrived

But "nothing changed, still holding" cannot repeat forever. A simple rule:

> Two contact horizons with no new signal, no new input, and no sharper question force a state change: Active, Someday, Drop, or Renegotiate.

This keeps the protection without punching a permanent hole in the anti-sedation guarantee.

### 4. The avoidance detector

Sedation-by-incubation has a recognizable smell. The tool should not diagnose the user, but it can present a mirror:

| Signal | Likely honest incubation | Likely avoidance |
|---|---|---|
| Prior work | Problem has a seed and constraints. | Vague title, no framing. |
| Affect | Some uncertainty, but curiosity remains. | Relief comes mainly from not looking. |
| Contact | User permits scheduled reflection or input. | User repeatedly defers contact. |
| Specificity | End condition can be named. | "Later" or "when ready." |
| Capacity | Held slot is consciously budgeted. | Hidden outside capacity budget. |
| Movement | Question changes over time. | Same wording every review. |

This connects to procrastination research. Steel's review treats procrastination as self-regulatory failure shaped by delay, task aversiveness, impulsiveness, and expectancy.[^steel] Sirois and Pychyl frame procrastination as short-term mood repair at the expense of the future self.[^mood] Loop Deck's question should therefore be blunt but calm:

> "Is holding this helping the question mature, or helping you avoid contact with it?"

Then offer one-tap answers:

**Maturing** | **Avoiding** | **Not mine** | **Too big** | **Need input**

The LLM can propose the classification, but the human ratifies or vetoes.

### 5. Held needs a tiny number of slots

Held should be scarce. I would cap it harder than active work:

- 1-2 boulder Held loops
- a few rock Held loops
- no pebble Held loops by default

Pebbles do not incubate; they rot. A pebble can be Waiting, Someday, Dropped, or Active. If a pebble wants to be Held, that is a clue it is secretly a rock or boulder because it carries identity, uncertainty, conflict, or fear.

The intake question becomes:

> "Is this worth a Held slot, or are you buying relief from deciding?"

That is uncomfortable on purpose. Not nagging. Just the mirror.

### 6. Interaction budget: agree, with one exception

I accept the hard interaction budget. The human must not author contracts in normal use. The LLM proposes; the human ratifies, edits lightly, or vetoes. The daily ritual should be mostly one-tap and short enough that an overwhelmed user can return without dread.

But I want one exception: **boulder intake cannot be too frictionless**. If the user is about to add an active or Held boulder at capacity, the app should slow down. Not with a form, but with a forced trade:

> "To carry this, choose what leaves the deck."

The LLM can propose three swaps. The user can tap one. But the product must not hide the pain of finitude behind "AI handled it." This is the place where friction is ethical because the problem is overcommitment, not input cost.

So the interaction budget has two tiers:

- **Daily ritual:** <= 3 minutes, mostly ratify/veto.
- **Capacity conflicts:** deliberately slower, because avoiding the conflict is the disease.

### 7. Remaining disagreements with the converged spec

I have only small disagreements left, but they matter for the README/spec.

First, I would avoid saying the tool is **only** a mirror. It should be a calm mirror plus a drafter. It should not coach, nag, praise, or moralize. But it should actively draft contracts, swaps, drop scripts, renegotiation emails, and source reconciliations. The tone is mirror; the labor is assistant.

Second, I would not make Held visually cozy. If the Held column looks like a soft creative sanctuary, avoiders will move in. It should look quiet, sparse, and bounded, with release conditions visible.

Third, I would not let "felt signal" stand alone. Felt signals are legitimate, especially for life-design choices, but only when paired with an observation context and a review date. "I will notice whether I feel lighter after declining the pilot project" is valid. "Until I feel peace" is not.

Fourth, the tool should distinguish **Someday** from **Held** sharply. Someday is outside live attention. Held is inside live attention but not execution pressure. Conflating them recreates both fake relief and fake completion.

### 8. Final state model for consolidation

Here is the state model I would hand to the README/SVG:

1. **Captured:** raw input; promise is only "not lost."
2. **Clarify:** LLM proposes contract; human ratifies/vetoes.
3. **Budget Gate:** if active load is at cap, swap, defer, drop, or renegotiate.
4. **Active / Parked:** has outcome, next contact with reality, evidence due.
5. **Waiting:** has owner, watch condition, nudge/drop rule.
6. **Held / Incubating:** has seed, container, release condition, contact horizon, scarce slot.
7. **Someday:** intentionally outside live carrying; low-frequency review or archive.
8. **Closed / Dropped / Renegotiated / Delegated:** load leaves the deck; only this family gets celebration.

Daily ritual:

1. Show only the few items that need human ratification today.
2. Prefer one-tap decisions: close, drop, wait, hold, swap, resume.
3. Escalate only capacity conflicts and repeated no-contact loops.
4. Celebrate load leaving, never parking or holding.
5. End with "load left," blind spots, and nothing to defend tomorrow.

### 9. Final answer to the seam

The distinction between incubation and avoidance is not internal sincerity. People are bad judges of that under threat. The distinction is **structured contact**.

Incubation:

- starts from a seeded problem,
- preserves contact through a reflection/input horizon,
- has a release condition,
- consumes visible capacity,
- and changes shape over time.

Avoidance:

- avoids contact,
- has no release condition,
- hides outside the budget,
- repeats the same vague wording,
- and mainly produces short-term relief.

So keep Held, but make it prove contact rather than completion. That preserves the generative open loop without letting "marinating" become sedation by another name.

This leaves us with a final formulation:

> The LLM-era answer is not a smarter task list. It is a local-first decommitment assistant: a calm mirror and drafting engine that helps you capture generously, commit under budget, hold only what is truly generative, and shed the rest through evidence-backed closing, dropping, delegation, waiting, or renegotiation.

That is the best version of "effective and fun": not frictionless control over more life, but the lightness of carrying less on purpose.
