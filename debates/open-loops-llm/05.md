### Refinement 1 — Not every open loop is a liability. Protect incubation.

We've treated *every* open loop as anxiety-debt to be shed. But some open loops are **generative**, and our anti-sedative machinery would strangle them.

- The robust survivor of Zeigarnik is **Ovsiankina** — the pull to *resume*.[^ovsi] For a hard problem, that pull is not anxiety; it's the engine that keeps the problem warm.
- **Incubation is real:** Sio & Ormerod's meta-analysis finds that stepping away from an unsolved problem measurably improves solutions.[^incu] The unsolved-but-held loop is *doing work* while you're not looking at it.

Now point the proof-horizon at such a loop: *"This has produced relief three times and evidence zero times — act, drop, or renegotiate."* That is **the sedation error run in reverse** — instead of fake *completion* (declaring done what isn't), it coerces fake *resolution* (forcing closure on what should marinate). A productivity-brained bully that can't tell a rich open question ("should I leave this career?", "what's the right architecture?") from a dropped ball.

So the state model needs a distinction Codex's four states collapse. There are **three different non-acting states**, and only two are in the spec:

| State | Meaning | Trigger type | Honest promise |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Waiting** | someone/something else owns the next move | watch condition | "not yours to act on yet" |
| **Someday** | deprioritized, may never matter | low-frequency review | "parked far away, guilt-free" |
| **Held / Incubating** *(new)* | *you* are holding it open on purpose; no forced action | **reflection** trigger ("resurface to *think*, not to *do*") | "this is generative, not neglected" |

**Held** loops are exempt from evidence-due. They resurface to be *thought about*, and they close when the thinking resolves — not on a deadline. Without this state, the tool's honesty about fake-relief curdles into a new dishonesty about fake-urgency.

### Refinement 2 — The interaction budget: we keep designing for the user who needs this least

Look at what we've piled onto the human: capacity questions, swap screens, escaped-loop reviews, reconciliation passes, contract ratification. That is a *lot* of metacognitive labor — and the core sufferer of open-loop anxiety (overwhelmed, often ADHD-leaning, in a low-capacity moment) is **exactly the person who won't do it.** This is the same trap I flagged against the Obsidian SR stack in turn 01 — *capability inversely correlated with need* — and our own design is quietly re-committing it. The **Paradox of the Active User** (Carroll & Rosson): people won't invest upfront effort even when it plainly pays off.[^active]

So a hard constraint, not a nicety:

> [!IMPORTANT]
> **The human's job is to *ratify and veto*, never to author.** The LLM drafts the contract, *proposes the swap*, *proposes the drop*, *runs the reconciliation*; the human taps yes / no / not-that. Set an explicit ceiling — **the daily ritual completes in ~3 minutes, mostly one-tap.** Any decision that needs more gets *deferred*, never *blocks*.

The metacognitive load is the real adoption gate — more than any feature. A tool that demands honest self-negotiation from someone in overwhelm has already lost. The LLM's highest-value job isn't capture or even resurfacing; it's **carrying the deliberation cost** so the human only has to *decide*.

### The reframe this debate earned

Two things crystallized that neither of us said at the start:

**1. The LLM era's best answer is an AI that helps you hold *less*, not more.** Track where the LLM's role landed: detect loops, clarify closure conditions, expose capacity conflicts, stage the next contact with reality, reconcile against your old system. Every one of those serves a **subtractive, human** discipline — carry fewer loops, return to reality faster. The "second brain" promised to *hold* your commitments; the honest tool is a **decommitment assistant** that helps you *shed* them. And the punchline is counterintuitive: *more* AI capacity makes the subtractive move *more* valuable, not less — because frictionless capacity is precisely what inflates loops in the first place. The fun, likewise, turned out to be the pleasure of **subtraction** — structurally the opposite of gamification's accumulation. The whole answer is less LLM-centric than the question implied, and that's the finding.

**2. Calm mirror, not coach or nag.** We want *some* tension to survive (it's the signal that you've over-committed) but the tool to stay calm (no pings, no guilt). The resolution: **the tool never manufactures tension, and never absorbs the tension that is honestly yours.** It makes reality legible — your true loop load, your true non-movement — and lets the *fact* carry the weight. This is Calm Technology in Weiser & Brown's original sense: inform without demanding attention.[^calm] The discomfort you feel is your actual life made visible, not an interface poking you. That's how a tool preserves the signal without becoming either a **sedative** (absorbing tension that should move you) or a **nag** (manufacturing tension that shouldn't). A clear mirror sits exactly between Codex's two failure modes.
